## Learning objectives

By the end of this guide you will be able to:

- Identify any regular Spanish verb's conjugation family (`-ar`, `-er`, or `-ir`)
- Conjugate any regular verb in the imperfect for all six grammatical persons
- Recognize the trigger words that signal the imperfect tense in a sentence
- Pronounce all six forms of any regular imperfect conjugation, graded by your microphone
- Distinguish the imperfect (ongoing, repeated past) from the preterite (single, completed past)

## TL;DR

The Spanish **imperfect** tense describes <RoughNotation type="underline" color="#b91c1c">repeated, habitual, or ongoing</RoughNotation> actions in the past. To form it, you drop the infinitive's last two letters and add one of two ending sets: the `-ar` set or the shared `-er` / `-ir` set. There are only **three irregulars** in the entire tense (`ir`, `ser`, `ver`), and this guide focuses on the regulars.

<Callout type="insight" title="The whole tense in one sentence">
  Drop `-ar` and add **aba, abas, aba, ábamos, abais, aban**. Drop `-er` or `-ir` and add **ía, ías, ía, íamos, íais, ían**. That is the entire regular imperfect tense.
</Callout>

## Glossary

- <KeyTerm term="imperfecto">The Spanish past tense for ongoing, repeated, or habitual actions. Translates to English "used to ___" or "was ___ing".</KeyTerm>
- <KeyTerm term="pretérito">The Spanish past tense for single, completed actions at a specific moment. Translates to English simple past, "I ___ed".</KeyTerm>
- <KeyTerm term="infinitivo">The unconjugated dictionary form of a verb. In Spanish, every infinitive ends in `-ar`, `-er`, or `-ir`.</KeyTerm>
- <KeyTerm term="raíz (stem)">What is left of an infinitive after you drop the final `-ar`, `-er`, or `-ir`. For `hablar`, the stem is `habl-`.</KeyTerm>
- <KeyTerm term="terminación (ending)">The conjugated suffix you attach to the stem. The imperfect has six per family, one per grammatical person.</KeyTerm>
- <KeyTerm term="conjugar">To attach the right ending to a stem so the verb agrees with its subject (yo, tú, él, nosotros, vosotros, ellos).</KeyTerm>

## The two ending families

Every regular Spanish verb falls into exactly one of two imperfect conjugation patterns. The pattern is decided by the last two letters of the infinitive.

### Pattern 1: `-ar` verbs

<RoughNotation type="box" color="#b91c1c" padding={10}>
For any verb ending in `-ar`, drop the `-ar` and add: **aba, abas, aba, ábamos, abais, aban**.
</RoughNotation>

| Pronoun | Ending | Example with `hablar` (to speak) |
|---|---|---|
| yo | **-aba** | habl**aba** (I used to speak) |
| tú | **-abas** | habl**abas** (you used to speak) |
| él / ella / usted | **-aba** | habl**aba** (he/she/you used to speak) |
| nosotros | **-ábamos** | habl**ábamos** (we used to speak) |
| vosotros | **-abais** | habl**abais** (you all used to speak) |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | **-aban** | habl**aban** (they/you all used to speak) |

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the stress mark on nosotros">
  The `nosotros` form is the **only** `-ar` imperfect ending with a written accent: <RoughNotation type="circle" color="#b91c1c" padding={4}>ábamos</RoughNotation>. Without the accent, the stress would shift to the wrong syllable.
</Callout>

### Pattern 2: `-er` and `-ir` verbs (shared)

<RoughNotation type="box" color="#b91c1c" padding={10}>
For any verb ending in `-er` or `-ir`, drop the `-er` or `-ir` and add: **ía, ías, ía, íamos, íais, ían**.
</RoughNotation>

The two families share the same endings, so once you know one, you know both.

| Pronoun | Ending | `comer` (to eat) | `vivir` (to live) |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | **-ía** | com**ía** | viv**ía** |
| tú | **-ías** | com**ías** | viv**ías** |
| él / ella / usted | **-ía** | com**ía** | viv**ía** |
| nosotros | **-íamos** | com**íamos** | viv**íamos** |
| vosotros | **-íais** | com**íais** | viv**íais** |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | **-ían** | com**ían** | viv**ían** |

<Callout type="insight" title="Every `-er` / `-ir` ending has an accent">
  Unlike the `-ar` set, **all six** `-er` / `-ir` imperfect endings carry a written accent on the `í`. Forget the accent, and the word becomes ungrammatical.
</Callout>

The imperfect tense follows a small set of trigger words and phrases. Spotting one of these in a sentence is your green light to use the imperfect.

| Spanish trigger | English equivalent | What it signals |
|---|---|---|
| **siempre** | always | Habitual action |
| **todos los días** | every day | Recurring action |
| **cuando era niño / niña** | when I was a child | Past habitual state |
| **muchas veces** | many times | Repeated action |
| **a menudo** | often | Frequency without a specific count |
| **mientras** | while | Two ongoing actions in parallel |
| **de niño / de joven** | as a child / as a young person | Past life stage |
| **los lunes** (any weekday) | on Mondays | Habitual weekly schedule |

## Heads-up: only three irregulars

<Callout type="warning" title="Out of scope today, but worth noting">
  The Spanish imperfect has only **three** irregular verbs in the entire tense: `ir`, `ser`, and `ver`. Every other verb in the language follows the patterns on this page exactly. The three irregulars deserve their own focused study session.
</Callout>

## Mnemonics

- **"-ar wears a B, -er and -ir wear a stripe"**: every `-ar` imperfect ending has a `b` in it (a**b**a, a**b**as, ...). Every `-er` / `-ir` imperfect ending has a written accent on the `í`.
- **"Only nosotros gets the hat"**: in the `-ar` family, only the `nosotros` form has an accent (ábamos). Everywhere else in `-ar`, no accent.
- **"-er and -ir are twins"**: they share their endings exactly. Memorize one, and you know both.

## Common pitfalls

<Callout type="warning" title="The five mistakes that lose points on tests">
  1. **Forgetting the accent on `-ar` nosotros**: `hablábamos`, not `hablabamos`.
  2. **Forgetting the accent on every `-er` / `-ir` ending**: `comía`, not `comia`.
  3. **Mixing the families**: `-ar` verbs do NOT take `-ía` endings. Check the infinitive ending first, every time.
  4. **Confusing yo and él / ella / usted**: in both ending sets, these two forms are identical (`hablaba`, `comía`). Context tells you who is speaking.
  5. **Using preterite when the trigger word screams imperfect**: if you see `siempre`, `todos los días`, or `cuando era niño`, do not even consider the preterite.
</Callout>
